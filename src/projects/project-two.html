Overview

The T-base lathe is a precision machining project that reimagines the conventional lathe by aligning two linear axes on a single base. This reduces geometric error, simplifies calibration, and improves surface flatness for micro-scale facing operations.

Designed and built as part of MIT’s 2.77: FUNdaMENTALS of Precision Machine Design, the project explores how precision engineering principles can be applied using standard lab resources and student machining skills.

Work

Iterative prototyping refined the alignment and stiffness of the design. I modeled, machined, and assembled the components, then validated surface flatness and tool motion accuracy through profilometry and lead-screw calibration tests.

  • Modeled lead-screw motion and backlash compensation in CAD.
  • Machined aluminum components with tight tolerance control.
  • Implemented surface metrology for quantitative verification.

Conclusion

The final assembly demonstrated consistent motion precision and repeatable surface quality. The project strengthened my understanding of kinematic design, GD&T, and tolerance analysis—skills crucial for high-precision manufacturing and instrumentation.
